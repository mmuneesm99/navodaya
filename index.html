The Kakkanad Manifesto

Optics. Chemistry. Widescreen Dreams.

When Maliampurackal Chacko Punnoose (Navodaya Appachan) stepped away from Udaya Studios to build Navodaya on the isolated, rugged hills of Kakkanad in 1976, he was driven by a single conviction: Indian storytelling had outgrown the static confinement of painted studio flats. Widescreen cinema required actual mountains, massive reservoirs, and true stereoscopic cameras.

Navodaya did not simply shoot movies; they built the physical infrastructure behind them. When the studio decided to release the region's first anamorphic Cinemascope feature, they surveyed and modified theater seating and projection designs across South India to ensure audiences could fully experience the wide, panoramic horizons.

First Indian 70mm Film

Padayottam

Shot in true 70mm at a time when most Indian studios had never handled the gauge, Padayottam forced Navodaya's engineers to design their own optics, projection rigs, and processing chemistry from scratch.

Pioneering Milestones

Technological Achievements

Navodaya Studio's engineering records changed the dimensions of Malayalam and Indian cinema forever.

1978

Cinemascope

Introduced wide anamorphic projections to the regional screens with the costume epic Thacholi Ambu.

70mm

Film Gauge

Produced Padayottam, the first Indian film shot, blown up, and mixed entirely domestically in 70mm.

3D

Stereoscopy

Released My Dear Kuttichathan, building custom twin-lens projection rigs to alignment tolerance.

1992

Digital Edit

Pioneered non-linear systems by importing the first Avid editing suite and ProTools audio console to India.
