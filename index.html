The Kakkanad Manifesto

Optics. Chemistry. Widescreen Dreams.

When Maliampurackal Chacko Punnoose (Navodaya Appachan) stepped away from Udaya Studios to build Navodaya on the isolated, rugged hills of Kakkanad in 1976, he was driven by a single conviction: Indian storytelling had outgrown the static confinement of painted studio flats. Widescreen cinema required actual mountains, massive reservoirs, and true stereoscopic cameras.

Navodaya did not simply shoot movies; they built the physical infrastructure behind them. When the studio decided to release the region's first anamorphic Cinemascope feature, they surveyed and modified theater seating and projection designs across South India to ensure audiences could fully experience the wide, panoramic horizons.

Interactive Exhibition

The Kakkanad Gravity Room

In 1984, to capture the gravity-defying sequences of My Dear Kuttichathan, Navodaya Appachan and director Jijo Punnoose engineered a fully rotational cubic set suspended on a massive steel axle.

By bolting the camera directly to the rotating room frame, the set appeared upright and stationary, creating the illusion of children walking up walls and dancing on ceilings. Use the interactive simulator to toggle perspectives and see the engineering behind the magic.

First Indian 70mm Film

Padayottam

Shot in true 70mm at a time when most Indian studios had never handled the gauge, Padayottam forced Navodaya's engineers to design their own optics, projection rigs, and processing chemistry from scratch.

Pioneering Milestones

Technological Achievements

Navodaya Studio's engineering records changed the dimensions of Malayalam and Indian cinema forever.

1978

Cinemascope

Introduced wide anamorphic projections to the regional screens with the costume epic Thacholi Ambu.

70mm

Film Gauge

Produced Padayottam, the first Indian film shot, blown up, and mixed entirely domestically in 70mm.

3D

Stereoscopy

Released My Dear Kuttichathan, building custom twin-lens projection rigs to alignment tolerance.

1992

Digital Edit

Pioneered non-linear systems by importing the first Avid editing suite and ProTools audio console to India.

The Engineering Minds

The Kakkanad Engineering Desk

Before Navodaya built the films, they built the physical machinery. Select an expert below to highlight their corresponding optical focus in the schematic drawings.

Set Engineering & Logistics

M. Chacko Punnoose (Appachan)

The founder and physical architect of Navodaya Kakkanad. Appachan pioneered outdoor widescreen staging and logistics, designing the horizontal steel structural mountings and mechanical pivot axles that made rotating sets like the gravity room possible.

3D Stereoscopy & Optics

Jijo Punnoose

Lead visual researcher who brought twin-lens 3D stereoscopy to India. Jijo designed the dual-camera optical alignment paths for shooting fantasy films and calculated projection geometry constraints to align dual projection lenses in local theaters.

Digital Audio & Editing

Jose Punnoose

Technological modernizer who led the digital audio initiative in 1992. Jose spearheaded the installation of India's earliest non-linear editing platform (Avid Media Composer) and digital multi-track audio console (ProTools), replacing manual tape-splicing gates.

Chemistry & Widescreen Film

Stereo Lab Technicians

The chemical engineers behind the 70mm gate print chemistry. They calculated chemical bath timings and optical blow-up lenses to process wide 70mm and stereophonic prints entirely inside Navodaya's Kakkanad laboratories.
