Chapter I

Foundations of Stereoscopy

Fig. 01Binocular convergence — two viewpoints reconciled into one point of depth.

Long before a single frame of Kuttichathan was exposed, Navodaya's technical unit spent three years studying binocular disparity — the small difference between what a left and right eye perceive independently. Human depth perception relies on the brain reconciling these two offset images into a single spatial impression. To achieve this, Jijo Punnoose—a physics graduate who led the studio's technical division—traveled to the United States to research stereoscopic camera configurations and source specialized twin-lens optical rigs.

The studio's engineers built their earliest test rigs from paired 16mm cameras bolted to a shared plate, manually adjusting lens spacing by millimetres between takes and projecting the results onto a bedsheet to judge comfort. By establishing a strict "parallax budget" to keep left-and-right offsets within comfortable optical limits, Navodaya successfully eliminated the convergence eye strain that had historically made 3D a passing novelty elsewhere.

Chapter II

3D Imaging Principles

Fig. 02Dual-strip capture — left/right negatives held in mechanical sync.

My Dear Kuttichathan was shot using a custom dual-strip anaglyph process, with two synchronized negatives recording the left and right eye images in complementary color channels. Getting both strips to run in perfect mechanical sync — down to a fraction of a frame — was the difference between an image that read as depth and one that simply read as blur.

Beyond optical alignment, the production required unprecedented practical staging. Jijo designed a massive rotating octagonal set—constructed in Kochi with engineering assistance from Steel Industries Kerala. By rotating the entire room structure in sync with a fixed camera rig, Navodaya created the gravity-defying sequences (such as characters walking on walls and ceilings) live on stereoscopic lenses, avoiding digital effects entirely.

Projection required its own invention: a modified twin-lens projector housed at the back of participating theatres, calibrated afresh for each auditorium's throw distance. Navodaya designed the twin-lens alignment rigs and distributed paper red-and-cyan glasses at the doors, providing a low-cost decoder for a complex engineering challenge.

Jijo Punnoose's original design schematic for the 3D rotating set used to create the gravity illusion in My Dear Kuttichathan.

Chapter III

70mm Frame Geometry

Fig. 03Negative area — 70mm against the standard 35mm frame.

A standard 35mm frame gave Navodaya roughly half the negative area they wanted for Padayottam's water-borne set pieces. Moving to 70mm meant a frame nearly four times the surface area, which in turn meant redesigning gate tolerances, pull-down mechanisms, and even the studio's own film transport paths to handle film stock that was both wider and heavier through the camera.

Unlike contemporary widescreen epics in India that sent their negatives to overseas laboratories (such as London) for blow-up work, Padayottam completed its entire 70mm processing and 6-track stereophonic sound integration domestically. The commercial and technical success of the film was so profound that it catalyzed the conversion of over 1,100 theater screens across Kerala to support anamorphic widescreen projection formats.

Chapter IV

Sound & Digital Engineering

Fig. 04Six discrete channels — dialogue, effects, and score kept apart.

Image depth without matching sound depth felt hollow in test screenings, so Navodaya's audio team built a six-track magnetic mix for Padayottam, positioning dialogue, effects, and score across discrete channels rather than folding everything into a single mono strip.

In 1992, Jose Punnoose bypassed analog barriers by purchasing the very first Avid non-linear digital editing system and ProTools audio suite sold outside the United States. Navodaya set up free educational workshops in Kochi and Chennai, training hundreds of regional editors, directors, and technicians to transition from mechanical Moviolas to digital timelines, establishing the foundation of digital post-production in South India.

The historic Avid AudioVision unit setup at SS Digital/RealImage, which revolutionized non-linear sound post-production in South India.
