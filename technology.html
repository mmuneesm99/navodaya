Chapter I

Foundations of Stereoscopy

Fig. 01Binocular convergence — two viewpoints reconciled into one point of depth.

Long before a single frame of Kuttichathan was exposed, Navodaya's technical unit spent three years studying binocular disparity — the small difference between what a left and right eye independently perceive. Human depth perception relies on the brain reconciling these two offset images into a single spatial impression, and any camera system attempting to reproduce that illusion has to first solve for interaxial distance, convergence, and parallax budget before a single foot of film is loaded.

The studio's engineers built their earliest test rigs from paired 16mm cameras bolted to a shared plate, manually adjusting lens spacing by millimetres between takes and projecting the results onto a bedsheet to judge comfort. Most early attempts caused the kind of eye strain that had made 3D a novelty rather than a craft elsewhere in the world — the question Navodaya set out to answer was whether it could be made to feel unremarkable instead of spectacular.

Chapter II

3D Imaging Principles

Fig. 02Dual-strip capture — left/right negatives held in mechanical sync.

My Dear Kuttichathan was shot using a custom dual-strip anaglyph process, with two synchronized negatives recording the left and right eye images in complementary colour channels. Getting both strips to run in perfect mechanical sync — down to a fraction of a frame — was the difference between an image that read as depth and one that simply read as blur.

Projection required its own invention: a modified twin-lens projector housed at the back of participating theatres, calibrated afresh for each auditorium's throw distance. Paper anaglyph glasses, tinted red and cyan, were distributed at the door — a low-cost decoder for a genuinely high-cost engineering problem.

Jijo Punnoose's original design schematic for the 3D rotating set used to create the gravity illusion in My Dear Kuttichathan.

Chapter III

70mm Frame Geometry

Fig. 03Negative area — 70mm against the standard 35mm frame.

A standard 35mm frame gave Navodaya roughly half the negative area they wanted for Padayottam's water-borne set pieces. Moving to 70mm meant a frame nearly four times the surface, which in turn meant redesigning gate tolerances, pull-down mechanisms, and even the studio's own film transport paths to handle stock that was both wider and heavier through the camera.

The resulting image held detail at a scale Indian screens hadn't yet been built to show, so a parallel effort went into recalibrating projection throw and screen curvature at the handful of theatres capable of exhibiting the format at all.

Chapter IV

Sound & Digital Engineering

Fig. 04Six discrete channels — dialogue, effects, and score kept apart.

Image depth without matching sound depth felt hollow in test screenings, so Navodaya's audio team built a six-track magnetic mix for Padayottam, positioning dialogue, effects, and score across discrete channels rather than folding everything into a single mono strip.

In 1992, Navodaya bypassed analog sound barriers completely by introducing the Avid Digital Editing Suite and ProTools digital audio workstation. Equipping a Mac Quadra 900, editors Jijo and Jose trained local industry veterans to assemble sound effects and voice tracks non-linearly, ushering in the digital era before it took off in Hollywood.

The historic Avid AudioVision unit setup at SS Digital/RealImage, which revolutionized non-linear sound post-production in South India.
